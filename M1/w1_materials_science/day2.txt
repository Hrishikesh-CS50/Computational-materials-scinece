# SESSION 3 — Materials Science: Atomic Structure

## Professor-Style Explanation

This session is foundational. If you understand atomic structure, you understand **why materials behave the way they do**.

Everything in engineering materials—strength, conductivity, corrosion resistance, brittleness, semiconductors—starts at the atomic level.

---

# Topic 1: Components of the Atom

An atom has three primary particles:

| Particle | Charge | Location       | Role                             |
| -------- | ------ | -------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Proton   | +1     | Nucleus        | Defines the element              |
| Neutron  | 0      | Nucleus        | Adds mass, stabilizes nucleus    |
| Electron | -1     | Around nucleus | Controls chemistry & electricity |

## Think of it structurally:

* The **nucleus** is dense and central.
* Electrons exist in surrounding energy regions (orbitals), not simple circular paths.

## Critical Engineering Insight:

For mechanics, protons/neutrons mostly determine mass.

For materials behavior, **electrons matter most**.

---

# Topic 2: Atomic Number

## Definition

Atomic number = number of protons in the nucleus.

This is the identity card of the element.

Z = \text{Number of Protons}

If an atom has:

* 1 proton → Hydrogen
* 6 protons → Carbon
* 13 protons → Aluminum
* 14 protons → Silicon
* 29 protons → Copper

Then it must be that element.

## Why this matters:

If you change proton count, you change the element itself.

---

# Topic 3: Why Electrons Matter

This is where materials science begins.

## Electrons determine:

### 1. Bonding

Why atoms stick together.

* Metals → metallic bonding
* Ceramics → ionic/covalent bonding
* Polymers → covalent chains

Bonding controls:

* strength
* melting point
* stiffness
* brittleness

---

### 2. Conductivity

Can electrons move easily?

* Copper → yes (excellent conductor)
* Silicon → controlled movement
* Glass → poor conductor

---

### 3. Chemical Reactions

Atoms react by gaining, losing, or sharing electrons.

That means:

* corrosion
* oxidation
* battery chemistry
* coatings
* combustion

---

### 4. Semiconductor Behavior

Modern electronics depend on controlling electrons in Silicon.

That gives us:

* diodes
* transistors
* sensors
* CPUs
* solar cells

---

# Topic 4: Isotopes

## Definition

Same number of protons, different number of neutrons.

So they are the same element chemically, but different mass.

Example:

Carbon:

* Carbon-12
* Carbon-13
* Carbon-14

All have 6 protons.

## Why engineers care:

* nuclear materials
* radiation detection
* medical imaging
* dating materials
* tracer studies

---

# Topic 5: Scale Awareness

Typical atom diameter:

10^{-10}\ \text{m} \approx 0.1\ \text{nm}

That is incredibly small.

For perspective:

* Human hair ≈ 70 micrometers
* Atom ≈ 0.1 nanometer

A hair is hundreds of thousands of atoms wide.

## Why this matters:

Macroscale properties come from nanoscale structure.

Examples:

* crystal defects weaken metals
* grain boundaries strengthen alloys
* dopants control semiconductors

# Quick Check Questions

1. Why does copper conduct electricity well? Because copper has loosely bound outer electrons that can move easily through the metal lattice. In metals, many electrons are not attached to one single atom. They behave like a shared electron cloud.
When voltage is applied, these mobile electrons drift and carry charge.
Why copper specifically!
low electrical resistance
high electron mobility
stable material
ductile (easy to make wires)
corrosion resistant

That is why power cables, motors, transformers, and electronics use copper.

2. Why is silicon useful for electronics but not as a wire?  Because silicon is a semiconductor, not a conductor. Its electrons are not free like copper’s. They require added energy or doping to move.

This is exactly why it is valuable:
Engineers can control when it conducts and when it does not.
That allows creation of:
transistors
switches
logic gates
processors
memory chips
solar cells
Why not as a wire?
A wire should conduct all the time with low resistance. Copper does that. Silicon does not. Silicon is used where control is needed, not where continuous current flow is needed

3. Why does changing neutrons not change the element? Because an element’s identity is determined only by number of protons.
